# Writing Hooks That Stop the Scroll

Read this for any task about hooks: writing them, grading them, or building a social brief that needs them.

The internet is full of "100 viral hooks" lists, and the model has read all of them. Handing it another list changes nothing, because it can already generate "If I woke up broke tomorrow and wanted to be rich by Friday, here is what I would do." A template list also quietly pulls the model toward fill-in-the-blank hooks, which is the generic output we are trying to beat. So this file is not a list. It is the judgment underneath the lists: why a hook works, how to engineer one for a real situation, and how to refuse one that only looks like a hook.

## What a hook actually is

A hook is the brief's tension, compressed into the first one to three seconds and engineered to trigger an involuntary reaction before the viewer decides whether to scroll. It has two parts, and the model usually writes only the first:

- **The hook** stops the scroll.
- **The seed** is the line right after it that promises why staying is worth the time. A great hook with no seed wins the first second and loses the next five. Always write the hook and the seed together.

## The drivers: why a hook stops the scroll

A hook works when it fires at least one of these in the first beat. These are the physics. Every mechanism below is only a way to deliver one or more of them. When a hook is weak, it is almost always because none of these is actually firing: it is a statement, not a trigger.

1. **Curiosity gap** — opens a loop the brain is compelled to close. Promises information the viewer does not have and cannot guess. "The reason your [result] never sticks is not [the obvious thing]."
2. **Self-recognition** — the viewer thinks "this is about me." Names a specific person, age, situation, or pain so precisely they feel seen. Vague audiences kill this instantly.
3. **Pattern interruption** — breaks the expected rhythm of the feed. A jarring first frame, an unexpected object, a line that contradicts what everyone in the category says.
4. **Stakes** — something to gain or lose, right now. The tension made urgent. "You are losing [result] every day and do not realize it."
5. **Emotion** — it makes them feel something in the first second: shock, recognition, indignation, aspiration, relief. A hook that triggers no feeling is wallpaper.
6. **Specificity** — concrete numbers and details that feel true and earned. "$4,000 in nine days" beats "a lot of money fast." Specificity buys belief; vagueness signals a lie.

The test for any hook: name which drivers fire in the first beat. If you cannot name one, it is not a hook yet, no matter how polished it sounds.

## The mechanism palette

Mechanisms are ways to deliver the drivers. This is a palette to choose from, not a fill-in-the-blank list. For each: the drivers it pulls, and when to reach for it. One example each, to show the shape, never to copy. **Always rewrite specific to the real situation.**

1. **Curiosity / withheld secret** — curiosity, stakes. Opens a loop and refuses to close it in the hook. "Nobody tells you what [common thing] actually does to [outcome]." Strong cold, top of funnel.
2. **Contrast / side-by-side** — pattern interrupt, specificity. Two things next to each other so the gap does the work. "Her case: curated. Her charger: whatever was on Amazon." Great for consideration; exposes a gap.
3. **Before / after / transformation** — specificity, aspiration, authority. "This was my [thing]. This is it now." Proof stage; earns belief.
4. **Callout / qualification** — self-recognition, stakes. Names the exact viewer. "If you are [age] and still [pain], stop scrolling." Works anywhere; sharpens relevance.
5. **Hard truth / hot take** — pattern interrupt, indignation. The uncomfortable specific said out loud. "You have four phone cases and one ugly charger." Cold to consideration.
6. **Myth-bust / de-influence** — curiosity, pattern interrupt. Overturns a belief the viewer holds. "[Common advice] is a lie. Here is what actually works." Good for building trust.
7. **Results / proof** — specificity, curiosity. Leads with a concrete number and a method gap. "Here is exactly how much [X] you need to [result]." Consideration to close.
8. **Mistake / warning** — stakes, self-recognition. "You are doing [thing] wrong, and it is costing you [loss]." Educational; creates urgency.
9. **Story / confession** — emotion, self-recognition. Personal stakes that mirror the viewer. "Two years ago I [low point]." Builds trust; a slow burn, not a fast sell.
10. **Future pacing** — curiosity, aspiration. Collapses time. "It took me nine years. I will give it to you in sixty seconds." Strong promise; close.
11. **Pattern interrupt / shock** — pattern interrupt, emotion. A jarring open with no setup. "Don't touch this." Pure attention; needs a fast seed or it is a cheap trick.
12. **Mystery / open loop** — curiosity. A line that demands a "wait, what?" "If this finds you in the next three days, it is a sign." High stop rate, easy to overuse; the seed must pay it off.
13. **Challenge / question** — self-recognition, pattern interrupt. Dares the viewer, or asks the thing they cannot not answer. "Tell me your charger matches your case." Invites stitches and duets; consideration.
14. **POV / relatable** — self-recognition, emotion. Mirrors the viewer's exact reality. "POV: your charger ruins every flat-lay." Anywhere; pulls toward.

These map onto the four resolutions in `standard.md`: contrast, hard truth, and challenge tend to push **against**; transformation and relief **resolve**; callout and POV pull **toward**; myth-bust and identity stand **for**. The mechanism is the surface; the resolution is the structure underneath. Vary the mechanism across a matrix, but make sure the resolution fits the funnel stage (open the tension early, close it late; see `social.md`).

## How to write a hook

Do not start by picking a template. Start from the situation and engineer the drivers.

1. **Name the tension.** From the brief. No tension, no hook. (See `standard.md`.)
2. **Decide the funnel direction.** Cold, open the tension. Warm, close it. (See `social.md`.)
3. **Pick the driver you are firing.** Curiosity, self-recognition, stakes, emotion. Usually one or two, on purpose.
4. **Choose a mechanism** from the palette that delivers that driver and fits the direction.
5. **Make it specific.** Replace every generic noun with the realest, most concrete version: numbers, names, exact situations. This is the single biggest difference between a hook that lands and a template that dies.
6. **Make it feel something.** Read it back and name the emotion it triggers in the first second. If there is none, it is wallpaper. Rewrite.
7. **Write the seed.** The line right after the hook that promises why staying is worth it. Hook and seed ship together.

## Read or spoken: match the register

Everything above is built for scroll-stopping short-form, where a viewer decides in about a second whether to keep watching a feed. That is one register, and forcing its grammar onto a format where a real person is talking produces copy that sounds like an ad, not a person. Before writing a word, decide which register you are in.

**Read / scroll** — captions, static posts, paid social, a print headline. The viewer is scanning a feed. The hook devices above apply: cold-open on the payoff, front-loaded numbers, on-screen text, "POV:".

**Spoken / to camera** — a founder video, a voiceover, YouTube, a webinar, a podcast. A person is saying these words out loud to someone who already chose to press play. The rules change:

- Write how people actually speak. Contractions, natural cadence, one idea per breath. Read every line out loud, and if you would never say it that way, rewrite it.
- Give context before the payoff. On camera a viewer needs to know what they are looking at before a number or a reveal can land. A context-free cold open that works as a silent text card ("$2,716.") falls flat when a founder says it with no setup.
- The opener is the one sentence a stranger understands with zero prior context, not the most dramatic line. Earn the reveal, then deliver it.
- Drop the overlay devices from the words a person says. On-screen callouts, "POV:", and front-loaded shock lines are written-hook tricks; spoken aloud they sound off.

The test for anything that will be spoken: say it out loud. If it does not sound like something a real person would say, it is wrong, no matter how well it grades as text.

## Grading a hook

A hook fails if any of the first four is missing.

- **Driver** — does at least one real driver fire in the first beat, or is it just a statement? Name it.
- **Specific** — is it concrete to a real person and situation, or a generic template with the blanks barely filled?
- **Emotion** — does it make someone feel something in the first second?
- **Seed** — is there a promise right after the hook, or does it stop the scroll and then waste the attention?
- **Funnel fit** — does it open or close the tension in the direction the funnel stage needs?
- **Honesty** — does the hook write a cheque the content actually cashes? A curiosity or mystery hook that never pays off trains the audience to distrust you. Overpromising wins one view and loses the next, which is the difference between attention and a following.
- **Register** — will these words be read or spoken? Spoken copy has to sound like a person talking and give context before the payoff. A scroll-hook in a founder's mouth reads as an ad; say it out loud to check.

The most common failure is a hook that is grammatically a hook ("Nobody tells you...") but fires no driver, names no specific person, and triggers no emotion. Catch it every time. The shape is not the hook; the trigger is.

## Delivery: the hook is three hooks

On video the hook lands in three channels at once, and the model usually writes only one. A strong concept specifies all three, and they reinforce the same tension.

- **Verbal / text hook** — the words. Everything above.
- **Visual hook** — the first frame. Movement, an unexpected object, a pattern break, a before-state on screen. A static talking head is the weakest possible open.
- **Sound hook** — a sound that snaps attention in the first beat: a whoosh, a riser, a click, a register cha-ching.

When grading or building for video, ask what the first frame and the first sound are, not just the line.

## Retention after the hook

Briefly, because it decides whether the hook was worth writing. Hold attention with a cut roughly every two to three seconds (angle change, zoom, new location), and make sure the seed's promise is actually delivered before the end. A perfect hook on a flat, uncut, unpaid-off video still dies. The hook buys the first three seconds; the structure keeps them.
