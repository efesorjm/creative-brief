# The Social Layer

Read this for any social, platform, or hook request. A social brief runs on the same IIOB engine and the same tension logic as a standard brief. It adds a tactical layer on top: platform reality, and the hook. Apply the standard checks first, then everything here.

---

## Social brief vs standard brief

They are not rivals. The standard brief sets the strategy and the emotional core; the social brief is the tactical offshoot that makes it land on a platform.

| | Standard / campaign brief | Social brief |
|---|---|---|
| **Who reads it** | Internal creative team | Content creators, social strategists, community managers |
| **Core focus** | The human problem and one proposition | The platform-specific insight and the cultural moment |
| **Scope** | Campaign-level direction | Individual posts, series, native formats (video, static, UGC) |
| **On the hook** | The *what and why*: the thematic angle and emotional register | The *how*: the exact device that stops the scroll, per format |

The standard brief tells the team *what to say and why it matters*. The social brief tells them *how to stop a thumb in the first second and earn the next three*. A social brief still needs a real problem, a real insight, and a real tension underneath it. Skipping that and going straight to "make it punchy" is how you get content that is loud and forgettable.

---

## The hook is a tension resolved in the first 1–3 seconds

This is the whole idea, and it is why the social layer sits cleanly on top of the four moves. A hook is not a separate trick. It is the brief's tension, compressed into the opening beat of a video or the first line of a caption.

Everything from `standard.md` still holds. The hook does not have to fight something. It can resolve a friction, stand for something, or pull someone toward a better state, exactly like any other resolution. The only hard rule is that there must be a real tension under it. **A hook with no tension is just noise that happens to be loud.** The scroll stops for a charge, not for volume.

---

## Hooks

The hook is where a social brief lives or dies, and it has its own file: **read `references/hooks.md` whenever the task involves writing or grading hooks.** The short version, so the social logic holds together:

A hook is the brief's tension compressed into the first one to three seconds, engineered to fire a real psychological driver (curiosity, self-recognition, stakes, emotion, specificity) before the viewer decides to scroll. It is not a template to fill in; the model already knows every template, and a hook with the right shape but no driver firing is wallpaper. Every hook also needs a **seed**, the line right after that promises why staying is worth it, and on video it lands in three channels at once: a visual hook, a text hook, and a sound hook. `references/hooks.md` has the drivers, the mechanism palette, the writing method, and the grading rubric.

## Funnel position decides the direction of resolution

The single most useful rule in this layer. The same product, briefed at two funnel stages, should produce hooks that resolve in opposite directions.

- **Cold** (they do not feel the problem yet) → **open the tension.** Question, surprising statement, contrast, hard truth. Make them feel a gap they were ignoring.
- **Consideration** (comparing options) → **sharpen and reframe.** Contrast, hard truth, identity, POV. Make the gap specific and make their current choice feel incomplete.
- **Conversion / retargeting** (interested, hesitating) → **close the tension.** Relief, satisfaction, discovery, identity. Resolve the friction and reassure.

If a social brief does not state where in the funnel the person is, that is the first thing to fix, because it determines whether the hook should widen the gap or resolve it. Tied to this is **audience temperature**: a cold viewer has never met the brand, so the hook must create the problem and the curiosity from scratch; a retargeted viewer already has, so the hook can reference the exact thing they looked at and move them.

## The output: a hook matrix

Social runs on volume and testing, so a social brief produces several hooks against one angle, not a single line. Use this shape:

```
Angle: [the one-line creative angle for this concept]
Funnel position: [cold / consideration / conversion]   Audience: [cold / retargeting]

| Hook type | Copy | Why |
|-----------|------|-----|
| [type]    | "…"  | [what it does in one line] |
| [type]    | "…"  | [resolves a different way]  |
| …         |      |                             |

Why it scales: [the repeatable pattern beneath the hooks]
```

The matrix is strong when the hook types resolve the tension in genuinely different ways, and weak when they are four flavors of the same move. **Why it scales** is not optional: a strong social concept is a repeatable *pattern* ("Her X: curated, her Y: generic") that regenerates across products, colorways, formats, and creators. A pattern that only works once is a post. A pattern that regenerates is an engine. Name which one you are looking at.

## Building a social brief: the Five A's

When building (not just generating hooks), assemble the brief in this shape. It opens with one line and fills in five blocks.

**The one-liner:** Help **[this group]** to **[solve this problem]** by **[how we solve it]** in **[the formats and platforms]** and **[what success looks like]**.

1. **Ambition** — *Goal* (put a number on it) and *Dream* (put the heart into it).
2. **Audience** — what they are wrestling with that we can help with. This is where the insight and tension live.
3. **Assets** — the campaign idea if one exists, the *message* (what we need to say), the *proof* (what makes it true), the distinctive brand assets to use, *collabs* (creators, influencers), and the few hashtags that matter.
4. **Appearance** — *platforms* (where we appear), *trends* (what is working there now), *formats* (the shapes we take), and *attitude* (how we sound and behave).
5. **Action** — the *CTAs* (what we tell people to do) and *next* (where we send them).

Then attach the **hook matrix**. The Five A's set the strategy and the platform reality; the matrix is the tactical edge that makes it stop the scroll.

## GRADE: social brief

Run the standard IIOB and tension checks from `standard.md` first. Then layer these, and output them in the same graded shape (verdict, real problem, IIOB, where it goes lifeless, questions it can't answer, fix one thing), with a social block added:

- **Funnel position** — is it stated, and do the hooks resolve in the right direction for it (open if cold, close if converting)? A conversion brief leading with a cold-audience question is aimed wrong.
- **Hook = tension** — does each hook resolve a real tension in the first beat, or is it just loud? Name the ones that are volume without a charge.
- **Matrix variety** — do the hook types resolve in genuinely different ways, or is it the same move four times?
- **Platform-native** — does it use the format, trend, and attitude of the actual platform, or is it a TV ad in a vertical frame?
- **Does it scale** — is there a repeatable pattern, or one clever line that dies after one post?
- **Audience temperature and curiosity gap** — is it clear whether the viewer is cold or retargeted, and does each hook use a real mechanism (question, surprising statement, visual reveal)?

---

## BUILD: social brief

Interview as in the standard build, one or two questions at a time, but route through the social layer:

1. **Tension first.** Use the standard logic to land a real problem, insight, and tension before touching hooks. No tension, no hook worth writing.
2. **Funnel position and temperature.** Where is this person, and have they met the brand? This sets whether the hooks open or close the tension.
3. **Platform and format.** Where does it run, what is the native format, what is working there now, what attitude fits.
4. **Generate the hook matrix.** Four to six hooks against the angle, deliberately varying the resolution type. For each, write the one-line "why."
5. **Pressure-test each hook.** Two questions per hook: does it resolve the tension in the first beat, and does the pattern scale beyond this one post? Cut the ones that fail.
6. **Assemble** into the Five A's with the matrix attached, then grade the result honestly against IIOB and these social checks.

## Worked example: one product, two funnel positions

Same product as the `standard.md` teardown, a language-learning app whose tension is the gap between wanting to *speak* and being made to feel like a beginner with nothing to show for it. Watch the hooks resolve in opposite directions by funnel stage.

**Cold audience — open the tension**

```
Angle: The graveyard of quit attempts. Make them feel the pattern before we sell the fix.
Funnel position: cold   Audience: cold

| Hook type           | Copy                                          | Why |
|---------------------|-----------------------------------------------|-----|
| Hard truth          | "You have started Spanish four times."        | Specific, relatable, names the pattern |
| Question            | "Why do we quit every language at week two?"  | Opens a gap they cannot not answer |
| Surprising statement| "Vocabulary is the reason you cannot speak."  | Breaks the expected belief |
| Contrast            | "500-day streak. Zero real conversations."    | The gap does the work |

Why it scales: the pattern ("[impressive effort], [zero real result]") regenerates across any
language, any learner, any abandoned app. The quit story is universal.
```

**Retargeted audience — close the tension**

```
Angle: The first real conversation. Resolve the friction for someone who already looked.
Funnel position: conversion   Audience: retargeting

| Hook type    | Copy                                              | Why |
|--------------|---------------------------------------------------|-----|
| Relief       | "No streaks. No guilt. Just talking."             | Names the friction and dissolves it |
| Satisfaction | "That moment a stranger understands you. That."   | Sensory and emotional payoff |
| Identity     | "You would never fake an accent. Stop hiding in flashcards." | Flips their own standard |
| Discovery    | "The 10-minute habit that replaced my 500 streak."| The thing they did not know they needed |

Why it scales: the resolution pattern ("drop the [guilt mechanic], get the [real moment]")
regenerates across features and creators, and speaks straight to anyone we already pulled in.
```

Same product, same tension. The cold matrix widens the gap to make someone feel a problem; the retargeting matrix resolves it to move someone who already feels it. That direction-by-funnel-position is the heart of a social brief, and it is invisible to a model writing hooks on autopilot.
