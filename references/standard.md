# The Standard

Read this before grading or building a brief. The IIOB headings in `SKILL.md` are easy to nod along to and wave through. The difference between a pass and a fail on each one is specific, and this file is where that specificity lives.

---

## IIOB rubric

Judge every brief on four dimensions. For each, the question is not "is it present" but "would it actually do its job."

### Idea

**What it is.** A single clear thing we want a person to think, feel, or do. Not a summary of the campaign, not a list of deliverables, not a theme.

- **Pass** — One sentence. A stranger could read it and know what the work is trying to make someone think, feel, or do. It reads as a *response to the problem*, not a description of the output.
- **Weak** — A theme or a territory ("celebrate everyday moments"), or a feeling with no direction. Gestures at a vibe but does not tell anyone what to do with it.
- **Missing** — A description of the artefact ("a 30-second film and three social cutdowns") standing in for an idea. The brief names what gets *made* but not what gets *said* or *moved*.

**The tell.** Try to act on it. If you can picture five completely different campaigns that all satisfy it equally, it is a theme, not an idea. If the only thing it tells you is the format, the idea is missing.

### Insight

**What it is.** A real tension seen from the audience's side, not the brand's. Format: *people think X, but actually Y.* It has to carry tension and surprise you a little even though you already knew both halves.

- **Pass** — A turn that makes you go "huh, true." It reveals something the audience would recognize as true about themselves but would not say out loud. It is about *them*, not about the product.
- **Weak** — True but inert. A real statement about the audience that changes nothing because everyone already acts on it ("people are short on time").
- **Missing** — An observation wearing a suit, or a brand fact pretending to be an audience truth ("our users love our clean design"). No tension, no turn.

**The tell.** Say it out loud. If there is no second half, no "but actually," it is an observation. If the "insight" is a fact about the brand rather than a tension inside the person, it is not an insight at all. **This is the single most common failure in real briefs. Catch it every time.**

### Objective

**What it is.** An honest statement of what this work is actually for: emotional relevance, plain awareness, or preference. Pick one.

- **Pass** — One clear job, and the rest of the brief is consistent with it. If the objective is awareness, the brief is not secretly asking for a 12% conversion lift too.
- **Weak** — Two objectives stapled together ("build awareness and drive sign-ups") with no acknowledgement that they pull in different directions.
- **Missing** — A metric standing in for an objective ("hit 2M impressions"), which tells you the target but not what the work is trying to *do* to a human.

**The tell.** A brief that wants awareness *and* preference *and* conversion in equal measure usually achieves none, because the creative choices that serve one undercut the others. Make it choose.

### Benefit

**What it is.** A clear reason *this* person should care, in their terms.

- **Pass** — A reason a real person would repeat to a friend. Stated as something they get, not something the product has.
- **Weak** — A benefit that is really a feature in disguise ("powered by AI", "syncs across devices") with the "so what" left for the audience to figure out.
- **Missing** — No reason to care at all, or a reason that matters to the brand ("drives retention") rather than to the person.

**The tell.** Ask "so what" twice. If you hit a human payoff, it passes. If you hit another feature, it is weak. If you hit silence, it is missing.

## The four moves

These are what separate a brief that moves from one that merely informs. Probe them hardest in GRADE and demand them in BUILD.

### 1. Reframe the problem

The highest-leverage move, and the one almost nobody makes. Most briefs accept the problem they were handed. The craft is to question whether that is the real problem before anyone spends months solving it.

Always tie the reframe to the original ask with a defensible rationale: **"you told us X, we think the real problem is Y, and here is why."** Show you listened, then show the leap.

- *Handed:* "Awareness of our bank is low." *Reframed:* "People know we exist. They do not believe a bank could be on their side, so they never look closely. The problem is distrust, not awareness."
- *Handed:* "Sign-ups dropped after the redesign." *Reframed:* "Sign-ups are fine until the pricing screen. The problem is not the funnel, it is that the value is still abstract at the moment we ask for money."

A reframe without a rationale is a hunch. A reframe with one relocates the entire brief.

### 2. Observation vs insight

An observation is what everyone already knows. An insight is the turn underneath it, with tension in it.

- *Observation:* "Road accidents are caused by human error." True, and useless, because it changes nothing.
- *Insight:* "Everyone knows accidents are human error, but almost no one believes they are the dangerous one." The second half has tension. It surprises you even though you knew both halves.

Another:

- *Observation:* "People want to eat healthier." *Insight:* "People do not want to be healthy, they want to have been healthy. They want the result without the daily friction of the choice."

If the candidate insight has no second half, it is an observation. Show the person the turn it is missing rather than just failing it.

### 3. Find the tension, then resolve it

Every brief needs a real tension: a charge between how things are and how they could be. Without it, a brand is a soft shape floating in someone's head and the work has nowhere to go. But tension is not the same as an enemy, and the work does not always resolve by fighting something. The move is to read the tension first, then choose the resolution that is honest for this brand. There are several:

- **Against** — push against an enemy. Often not a competitor. A habit ("doing it in a spreadsheet," "calling the family accountant"), a belief ("investing is for people with money I don't have"), or a category convention you can break. Bottled water was always pure, healthy, natural; one brand made it *Liquid Death* and pushed against the entire category's politeness.
- **For** — stand for something and plant a flag, so people have a side to belong to. Patagonia does not mainly fight a competitor; it stands *for* the planet, and the tension is between consumption and conscience.
- **Resolve** — relieve a friction, take away the thing that was in the way. The tension is a daily annoyance, and the brand's move is to remove it. Much of the best product marketing lives here: name the friction everyone tolerated and dissolve it.
- **Toward** — name a better state and pull people toward it. The tension is the distance between who someone is and who they want to be, and the work closes it. Nike's "Just Do It" is not against an enemy; it pulls you *toward* the version of yourself that started.

Positioning is what the brand stands for and against. The brief's job is to carry a real charge and pick the resolution that fits.

**The failures to catch.** The first is *no tension at all*: a brief with nothing to resolve, only a description. The second is subtler and worth naming, because it is easy to commit: **reflexively reaching for a fight when the honest resolution is to stand for something, or to relieve a friction.** Not every brand has an enemy, and forcing one produces work that feels manufactured and combative. When grading, ask "is there a real tension, and is the brief resolving it in a way that is true to this brand," not "is there an enemy."

### 4. Kill the weak fast

A brief sets the gate for everything made after it. The discipline is not loving ideas, it is finding the reason a weak one fails and getting there quickly, while it is still cheap.

- In **GRADE**: name what is not working without softening it. A gentle critique is expensive, because it lets weak thinking survive into production where fixing it costs the most.
- In **BUILD**: do not let a weak answer live just because the user is attached to it. Surface the reason it fails, then ask again.

### Underneath all four: context

A brief that says only *what* to make, and never *where* it lands or *why someone is there*, is missing half the job. Attention without context is worthless and the channel is never neutral. Someone on TikTok keeping up, someone searching to act, and someone reading reviews for reassurance are three different people in three different states. A strong brief knows which one it is talking to and where.

## Worked example: lifeless vs moving

The same assignment, briefed two ways. Study the gap.

### The lifeless brief (the floor)

> **Project:** Q3 campaign, a language-learning app
> **Objective:** Increase brand awareness and drive sign-ups.
> **Audience:** Adults 25–45 who want to learn a new language.
> **Insight:** People are busy and want a convenient, flexible way to learn.
> **Message:** Learning a language has never been easier or more fun.
> **Deliverables:** Social, online video, display.

It is complete. Every field is filled. And it would produce flat work, because:

- **Idea — missing.** "Easier and more fun" is a description of the product, not a thing we want anyone to think, feel, or do. Five different campaigns could all claim it.
- **Insight — missing.** "People are busy and want convenience" is an observation. No second half, no tension, and every app in the category has already said it.
- **Objective — weak.** Awareness and sign-ups stapled together. The work that builds broad awareness is not the work that converts a download, and the brief has not chosen.
- **Benefit — weak.** "Easy and fun" is a feature with the "so what" missing. So what if it is easy?
- **Problem — accepted, not reframed.** It assumed the problem is that people do not know the app exists. It almost certainly is not.

### The moving brief (the ceiling)

> **Project:** Q3 campaign, a language-learning app
>
> **The problem.** It is not that people have not heard of us, and it is not that they are too busy. Almost everyone in our audience has already *started* learning a language and quit. The real problem is the graveyard of abandoned attempts, and the small private shame attached to each one.
>
> **Who.** Lapsed quitters who blame their schedule. They downloaded an app, kept a streak for nine days, felt like a beginner in front of no one, and stopped. They tell themselves they were too busy.
>
> **Insight.** People think they quit because they ran out of time. Actually they quit the moment the app made them feel like a beginner again, with nothing to show for it. The thing that beat them was private embarrassment, not the calendar.
>
> **Tension.** The gap between wanting to actually *speak* a language and being made to feel like a beginner with nothing to show for it. We resolve it two ways at once: we pull people **toward** the moment they sound like themselves, and we **relieve** the streak-guilt that beat them last time. In doing so we also stand **against** the category's shaming convention, but the charge is the pull toward real speech, not the fight.
>
> **Idea / proposition.** Make the first real conversation the hero, not the 500-day streak. *Sound like yourself, sooner.*
>
> **Get → To → By.** Get lapsed quitters who blame their schedule · To believe they can have one real conversation in weeks, not years · By replacing streak-guilt with proof of real speech, early.
>
> **Why they care.** You get to the part that actually feels like *you* — speaking, being understood — fast, instead of grinding vocabulary you will forget by Friday.
>
> **Objective.** Preference. We are not chasing awareness; we are changing which app a returning quitter trusts with their next attempt.

Same product, same budget, same brief fields. One informs. One reframes the problem, carries an insight with a real turn, finds a real tension and resolves it honestly, and lands on an idea a stranger could act on. That gap is the entire job of this skill.
